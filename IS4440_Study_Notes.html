Part 01 — What is Society?
🏛 Defining Society
Definition: "A society is a large social grouping that shares the same geographical territory and is subject to the same political authority and dominant cultural expectations."
GroupSociety is made of people — it is a human grouping, not simply a territory. Without people, there is no society even if the land still exists.
BoundaryA line separating members from non-members. It answers: "Who belongs here, and who does not?"
GateA controlled point of entry and exit within a boundary — may be very open or very strict.
Territory & AuthorityShared geographic space plus a political system or power structure that organises life in that space.
💡 Easy memory: Society = People + Rules + Place + Power + Shared expectations. It is a connected human world, not just "many people living near each other."
6 Key Properties of Society
01 Life Cycle

Societies are born (created by a person or group) and can die through collapse, merger, or dissolution. They are not permanent.

02 Membership

One person can belong to several societies simultaneously — a national, religious, professional, and online society all at once.

🔍 Example: a Sri Lankan software engineer who is also a gamer and a mosque member.

03 Behaviour

What do members do — and avoid doing? Sociology studies how member behaviour changes over time within and across groups.

04 Diversity

Societies range from homogeneous (similar members) to heterogeneous (diverse members). Diversity brings creativity and variation but also potential conflict and norm deviation.

05 Interaction

Societies interact with each other — sometimes cooperatively, sometimes in conflict. Members can also cross societal boundaries.

06 Non-human

Society includes non-human elements: animals, plants, machines, technology. Smartphones changed friendship, work, and politics — technology shapes society.

Boundary Types & Related Academic Areas
🔒 Types of Social Boundaries
Closed BoundaryTightly controlled — strict membership, little or no easy entry or exit (e.g., a secretive cult).
Gated BoundaryEntry and exit possible but regulated (e.g., professional licensing boards).
Swing / Saloon GateFluid — members can move in and out freely; societies can overlap and interconnect.
Overlapping SocietiesOne person belongs to multiple societies simultaneously — national, professional, online.
Internet SocietiesOnline spaces form real societies with roles: active arguers, influencers, silent observers.
⚠️ Common mistake: students think society = country. In reality, an online gaming guild, a professional body, and a family are all societies.
🎓 Related Academic Disciplines
SociologyStudies human society and social interaction — groups, structures, institutions.
PsychologyStudies individual behaviour and thinking. Focuses on the person, not the group.
AnthropologyStudies human cultures, evolutionary origins, and diversity across time and place.
ArchaeologyStudies material remains — objects and structures — from past human life.
EconomicsStudies how people satisfy needs through production, distribution, and consumption.
Political ScienceStudies government, voting, political parties, and civic behaviour.
💡 Soc vs Psych: One student fails → Psychology asks about that student's stress. Sociology asks about school inequality, class, and peer structure.

Part 02 — Sociological Imagination (C. Wright Mills)
🔍 Sociological Perspective
Sees general social patterns in individual lives. The experience of crisis or marginality sharpens this perspective — it enables us to connect personal experience to broader social forces.
  • Framework for understanding — reveals the complex interplay between individuals and society.
  • Identifying social problems — helps analyse phenomena and push toward positive social change.
  • The outsider view — people who feel excluded or who experience crisis often see society's patterns most clearly.
  • Connecting individual & society — personal troubles become public issues.
🔍 Example: Many graduates cannot find jobs → Sociology asks about the economy, education system, and labour market — not just individual effort.
💡 The Sociological Imagination
C. Wright Mills: A "quality of mind" that allows one to grasp "history and biography and the relations between the two within society."
BiographyYour personal life story — the individual experience.
HistoryThe wider events and conditions of your time — the structural context.
Personal TroubleA problem rooted in an individual's own life and character (private).
Public IssueA matter that transcends the individual and lies in the social structure.
⚠️ Mills criticised fact-collecting sociology that only served administrative decisions. He argued social science must address real, substantive problems and connect private troubles to public issues.
⚙️ Mills' 8 Principles of Intellectual Craft
1
Integrate Work & Life

Your experiences and thinking should enrich each other. Sociology is not a 9-to-5 job.

2
Keep a File

Collect ideas, observations, and reflections — a compendium of personal and intellectual experiences. Promotes organisation and writing discipline.

3
Continual Review

Keep rethinking your ideas — engage in ongoing review of thoughts, not only when forced by deadlines.

4
Learn from Bad Books

Even poor writing stimulates your mind by showing what is weak or missing. A bad book can be as intellectually stimulating as a good one.

5
Playfulness with Ideas

Maintain an attitude of playfulness toward phrases and concepts — combined with a fierce drive to make sense of the world.

6
Perspective of Others

Stimulate imagination by viewing the world from different viewpoints — historians, biologists, industrialists.

7
Imaginative Extremes

Do not fear bold, speculative thinking at early stages of inquiry — push ideas to their logical limits.

8
Simple, Direct Language

Express ideas as simply and directly as possible. If you cannot say it plainly, you do not understand it well enough.

"The sociological imagination enables its possessor to understand the larger historical scene in terms of its meaning for the inner life."
— C. Wright Mills, The Sociological Imagination (1959)

Part 03 — Socialisation & Personality
🧬 What is Socialisation?
Definition: Socialisation is the lifelong social experience by which people develop their human potential and learn culture — the process by which we become social beings.
Lifelong ProcessDoes not stop in childhood — continues through adulthood and old age as we enter new roles and environments.
Human PotentialThrough socialisation, individuals develop language, thought, emotion, and complex social interaction.
Culture TransmissionValues, norms, beliefs, and practices pass from one generation to the next — society teaches its way of life to new members.
Personality FormationMuch of personality is learned socially, though the exact balance between biology and environment is debated (Nature vs Nurture).
🔍 Example: Saying "thank you," respecting elders, dressing formally in work settings — all learned through socialisation.
⚖️ Nature vs. Nurture
Nature — BiologicalNurture — Sociological
Darwin / Instinct: Unlearned behaviours coded biologically — hunger, fear, reproduction.Behaviourism (Watson): Environment and conditioning determine personality, not genetics.
Heredity: Genetic inheritance shapes predispositions for temperament and intelligence.Socialisation: Lifelong learning of culture shapes who we are and what we value.
Freud's Eros/Thanatos: Tension between love/life instinct and death/aggression instinct.Application: Raises questions about animals, AI, and robots — can they be socialised?
⚠️ Common mistake: Nature and nurture are not enemies — personality is strongly socialised but still influenced by biology.
🔍 Example: Energetic child → environment determines whether that becomes leadership, athletics, or disruption.
👨‍🏫 6 Key Theorists of Human Development

01 — Jean Piaget

Cognitive Development

Four stages of cognitive development in children — sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, and formal operational. Intelligence grows through active engagement with the environment.

02 — Sigmund Freud

Psychosexual Development

Personality develops through stages driven by the tension between Eros (love/life) and Thanatos (death/aggression). The id, ego, and superego form through socialisation.

03 — Lawrence Kohlberg

Moral Development

Six stages of moral reasoning progressing from obedience to universal ethical principles. Moral development requires social interaction and cognitive growth.

04 — Carol Gilligan

Gender & Moral Development

Challenged Kohlberg's male-centred model. Women's moral reasoning emphasises care, relationships, and context rather than abstract rules and justice.

05 — G.H. Mead

Theory of the Social Self

The self develops through social interaction — the "I" (spontaneous self) and the "me" (socialised self). Language and role-taking are central to self-development.

06 — Erik H. Erikson

Eight Stages of Development

Personality develops across eight life stages, each presenting a psychosocial crisis (e.g., trust vs mistrust, identity vs confusion) that must be navigated.

Part 04 — Sociology as a Science
📖 Core Definitions
Sociology"The systematic study of human society and social interaction." Systematic = organised and method-based, not guesswork.
SocietyLarge social grouping sharing territory, political authority, and dominant cultural expectations.
SociologistA researcher who uses sociological methods to study and explain social phenomena.
Soc. PerspectiveSees general social patterns in individual lives; connects biography to history.
Micro LevelSmall-scale interaction between individuals (e.g., teacher–student conversation).
Meso LevelOrganisations and mid-level groups (e.g., a school as an institution).
Macro LevelSociety-wide structures and institutions (e.g., the national education system).
🔬 The 6-Step Scientific Method
1
Review Literature

Read what others already discovered — understand background, debates, and gaps.

2
Formulate Hypothesis

Create a testable, falsifiable prediction about variables. Example: "Students in peer groups report lower exam stress."

3
Choose Research Design

Select method — survey, experiment, ethnography, content analysis — best suited to the question.

4
Gather Data

Collect evidence through observation, interviews, questionnaires, records, or experiments.

5
Analyse Data

Use quantitative (statistical) or qualitative (interpretive) methods to identify patterns.

6
Draw Conclusions

Accept, reject, or refine the hypothesis. Publish results; invite replication and further research.

❓ Is Sociology a Science? — The Debate
Sociology employs the scientific method, but whether it is truly a science remains open.
Arguments FOR ScienceArguments AGAINST
Uses systematic, empirical methodsSocial phenomena are complex and hard to control
Generates falsifiable hypothesesResearcher values can influence findings
Builds cumulative theoretical knowledgeHuman subjects cannot be ethically experimented on at scale
Applies quantitative and qualitative rigourSocial meaning cannot always be measured objectively
Why Did Sociology Emerge?
Industrial RevolutionNew class structures, economic change, and social questions demanded systematic study.
Growth of CitiesPoverty, crime, disease, anonymity, and overcrowding required explanation.
Questioning TraditionPeople began rejecting old roles and religious authority — new thinking entered.
Scientific RevolutionSuccess of natural science inspired scholars to study society with the same rigour.

Part 05 — Moral Philosophy: The Runaway Trolley (Michael Sandel)
🚋 What is a Thought Experiment?
A thought experiment is an imagined situation used to test our moral beliefs more clearly — by removing real-world uncertainty so that only the moral choice stands out.

The trolley problem, developed by Michael Sandel in Justice: What's the Right Thing to Do?, forces us to examine competing moral principles. It does not give one final answer — its job is to force careful thinking about hidden moral rules in our own minds.

1️⃣ Scenario 1: The Trolley Driver
Situation: You are the driver. Brakes have failed. Main track → 5 workers die on impact. Side track → 1 worker dies. Can you turn?
Main track: Trolley ──────────► 5 workers
               \
                \
Side track:     ──────────► 1 worker
Most people say"Turn! Tragic though it is to kill one, it's worse to kill five." Sacrificing one life to save five seems morally justified.
Your roleYou are already involved as the driver — not an outsider.
ActionTurn a steering wheel — indirect physical act.
Moral verdict✓ Most say: RIGHT
2️⃣ Scenario 2: The Bridge Bystander
Situation: You stand on a bridge with a heavy man. Pushing him onto the track stops the trolley and saves 5 workers — but he dies. Same 5-vs-1 math.
      You + Heavy Man on bridge
                  │
                  ▼
Track: Trolley ──────────► 5 workers
Most people say"Of course not. It would be terribly wrong to push him." Pushing feels deeply, instinctively wrong despite the same numbers.
Your roleYou are a bystander — not originally involved.
ActionPush a person with your bare hands — direct physical act.
Moral verdict✗ Most say: WRONG

💡 The moral puzzle: Same numbers. Different moral reaction. Why? This shows moral judgment depends on more than simple counting of lives.
01 Using a Person as a Means

Pushing the heavy man turns him into a tool — his body is used to stop the trolley. The side-track worker is an unfortunate side effect of diverting the trolley, not a tool.

Strong argument — but both victims are used without consent.

02 The Role of Intention

In both cases, the death is foreseen, not strictly intended. If the heavy man somehow survived but still stopped the trolley, we would feel relief — suggesting his death is not the final goal.

Weakens the distinction — intention may not differ after all.

03 Physical Directness

Pushing with bare hands feels viscerally different from turning a wheel. Physical intimacy of the act triggers a stronger moral response — even if the outcome is the same.

Psychological reality — but is it a valid moral distinction?

🚪 The Trap-Door Twist — Testing the Real Principle
New scenario: Instead of pushing the heavy man, you open a trap-door by turning a steering wheel — he falls onto the track with no physical contact. Same outcome: he dies, five workers are saved.

If pushing is wrong because of physical contact…

…then opening the trap-door should be fine. You're just turning a wheel, just like the trolley driver. No hands on the man.

→ Suggests "pushing" is just squeamishness.

If the trap-door still feels wrong…

…then the real issue is not physical contact, but deliberately causing someone's death — using them as a means to an end — regardless of how we do it.

→ Suggests a deeper moral principle at work.

⚖️ Conflicting Moral Principles

Two valid principles clash: (1) save as many lives as possible and (2) never kill an innocent person even for a good cause. The trolley forces us to choose which takes priority.

🔍 The Value of Hypotheticals

By removing real-world uncertainty — we know exactly who dies — thought experiments let us examine moral principles more clearly than real dilemmas ever could.

🪞 Moral Reasoning as Self-Discovery

We often think moral reasoning is about persuading others. But it is also a way of examining our own convictions — figuring out what we believe and, crucially, why we believe it.

"Moral reasoning is not just a way of persuading others — it is a way of sorting out our own moral convictions."
— Michael Sandel, Justice: What's the Right Thing to Do?

Part 01 — Status
🏅 Meaning of Status
Status is a position a person occupies in a group or society — denoting not only the position itself but the relative social standing on a publicly recognised scale or hierarchy of social worth.
General MeaningA position a person occupies in a group or society. Denotes relative social standing on a publicly recognised hierarchy of worth.
Sociological MeaningThe amount of honour or prestige a person receives from members of the community in a stratification system.
Status vs RoleStatus refers to what a person IS; Role refers to the behaviour expected from that position.
"Status is a position in a social system, such as child or parent."
— R. Linton, 1936
📖 Multiple Definitions of Status
LintonStatus is the position in the general institutional system, recognised and adopted by the entire society — spontaneously evolved, rooted in folkways and mores.
Benoit-SmullyanStatus is the position that determines for its possessor, apart from personal attributes, a degree of respect, prestige and influence.
ZnanieckiStatus means the location of the individual within the group — their place in the social network of reciprocal obligations, privileges, rights and duties.
SimplifiedThe simplest definition: status represents the position of the individual in the group.
⚙️ Characteristics of Status
  • Determined by the cultural situation of society.
  • A relative concept — defined in relation to other members of the group.
  • Every individual plays roles in accordance with their status.
  • Every status carries associated rights and obligations of membership.
🔀 Types of Status

Ascribed Status

Assigned at birth or different life-cycle stages. Not chosen — determined by sex, age, kinship, caste, or race.

Determinants: Sex · Age · Kinship · Caste · Racial Differences

Example: Being born male or female; belonging to a particular ethnic group.

Achieved Status

Acquired during one's life. Chosen by the individual through effort and accomplishment.

Determinants: Education · Occupation · Politics · Physical Strength · Specialisation

Example: Becoming a professional athlete, doctor, or college graduate.

Part 02 — Role
🎭 Meaning of Role
A role is the expected behaviour associated with a specific social status. As Linton stated: a role is the totality of all the culture patterns associated with a particular status — it is the dynamic aspect of status.
Def. 1Role is the social action or behaviour of the individual in a particular status.
Def. 2Role is the manner in which the position of a status is supposed to be filled.
Def. 3All social activities and actions are organised around roles.
Def. 4Role is the set of privileges and expectations — both of one's own behaviour and the behaviour of others toward that position.
⚙️ Characteristics of Role
  • Roles change in accordance with social values, norms, and patterns. What is justified at one time may not be at another (e.g., historical practices).
  • Every role involves various types of action performed per social expectations. Every social role has a cultural basis.
  • Roles are fundamentally dynamic — they evolve over time as society changes.
⚡ 1. Role Conflict

One person holds contradictory roles that pull in different directions simultaneously.

🔍 Example: A nurse who must be both compassionate and clinically detached.

⚡ 2. Role Disagreement

Different people hold conflicting expectations of the same individual.

🔍 Example: The dean expects one thing from a lecturer, faculty another, and the union yet another.

⚡ 3. Inter-role Conflict

Conflict between multiple roles held by one person simultaneously — competing obligations.

🔍 Example: Someone who is both a spouse and a deployed soldier — family vs duty.

Part 03 — Norms & Values
📏 What are Norms?
Social rules that specify appropriate and inappropriate behaviour in given situations — shared standards of desirable behaviour that enable people to anticipate how others will interpret and respond to their words and actions.
FolkwaysEveryday customs and informal norms — being considered rude rather than criminal. Examples: holding the door, queuing, table manners. Weak enforcement.
Mores(Pronounced "more-ay") Stronger norms — violations cause revulsion and strong social condemnation. Almost universally condemned. Examples: incest taboos.
LawsFormal standards enacted by legislatures and enforced throughout society. The strongest form of norm — violations backed by state power and formal punishment.
📊 Severity scale: Folkways (mild) → Mores (strong) → Laws (strongest) — increasing severity of violation and sanction.
⚙️ Characteristics & Functions of Norms

Characteristics

  • Universal across all societies
  • Linked to the factual social order
  • Incorporate value judgements
  • Relative to situations and groups
  • Not always obeyed by all members
  • Normally internalised by people

Functions

  • Assist survival — protect vulnerable members
  • Guide everyday behaviour
  • Permit efficient social functioning
  • Give cohesion and solidarity to society
  • Promote self-control and discipline
🏆 Sanctions — Rewards & Punishments
A sanction is a prize or punishment received when you either abide by a norm or violate it. Sanctions reinforce values by rewarding conformity and punishing deviation.
Positive (Reward)Negative (Punishment)
Formal Awards, medals, scholarships, grants, public recognition, promotion Fines, imprisonment, job termination, legal action
Informal Smiles, compliments, applause, social approval, praise from peers Frowns, gossip, exclusion, ridicule, social disapproval
💎 What are Values?
A value is a belief that something is good and desirable — defining what is important, worthwhile, and worth striving for. Values are ideas about what is GOOD and BAD in a society.
Def. 1A conception or standard by which things are compared and approved or disapproved — held to be relatively desirable or undesirable.
Def. 2Socially approved desires and goals internalised through conditioning, learning or socialisation — becoming subjective preferences, standards and aspirations.
⚙️ Characteristics & Functions of Values

Characteristics

  • Abstract in nature
  • Unconsciously upheld
  • Emotionally based
  • Both positive and negative values exist
  • Subject to change over time
  • Specific to cultural context

Functions

  • Provide goals or ends for social life
  • Give direction and purpose
  • Provide uniformity in group behaviour
  • Give legitimacy to rules and norms
  • Give moral authority to social norms
  • Aid in conflict resolution between norms
  • Provide stability in social interaction

Part 04 — Culture
🌐 What is Culture?
"A toolkit for the survival of mankind."
Culture is the knowledge, values, customs, and material objects passed from one person, group or society to another.
  • All humans have a culture — it is the defining feature of human society.
  • Society and culture are interdependent (symbiotic) — neither could exist without the other.
  • For a society to survive there must be laws, rules, and guidelines — all of which are culture-driven.
  • Some cultures vary widely. The 'OK' sign means "I kill you" in Tunisia — context is everything.
💡 Why culture matters: Humans are not born with the information necessary to survive — we are shaped by NURTURE, not just Nature. Culture channels and shapes our reflexes and drives.

💬 Language: The Foundation of Culture
Language is a system of speech and/or written symbols used to convey meaning and communicate. About half of the world's languages are endangered — lost to conquest, commerce, or failure to write them down.
  • Language and thinking are directly connected — if you have no word for a concept, you may lack the ability to think it.
  • Language shapes social perceptions — terms like "chairman" or "mankind" encode gender assumptions.
  • Language is the primary vehicle of culture transmission across generations.

XenophobiaFear and hostility toward people from other countries or cultures.
Cultural RelativismUnderstanding a culture on its own terms, without judging it as superior or inferior — the ideal sociological stance.
XenocentrismThe belief that another culture is superior to one's own — the opposite of ethnocentrism.
Types of Culture

The values and standards a society professes to hold — what it aspires to be.

🔍 Example: Aspiring to full equality and freedom for all citizens.

🔄 Real Culture

The way people actually behave — not always matching the ideal.

🔍 Example: Claiming to value equality while minorities face systemic disadvantage.

A subset of the dominant culture with distinct values, beliefs, norms, language (argot), and rituals — but whose values coexist within the dominant culture.

✊ Counterculture

A subculture whose values run in opposition to the dominant culture.

🔍 Examples: Hippies of the 1960s, punk movement, Amish separatists.

🤝 Multiculturalism

Supports the inherent value of different cultures in a society — celebrating diversity rather than demanding homogeneity.

➡️ Assimilation

The process by which minority groups adapt to and adopt the norms and values of the dominant culture — willingly or under pressure.

Part 05 — Cultural Change & Sociological Perspectives
💡 Discovery

When a culture gains a new understanding of something already existing — recognising a natural law, medical treatment, or social pattern. Knowledge increases.

Example: Discovering penicillin; recognising that washing hands prevents disease.

🔧 Invention

Creating something new within a culture — a new technology, art form, or social institution.

Examples: The wheel, the printing press, the internet — each transformed society.

🌊 Diffusion

The spread of cultural elements from one group to another. Cultural levelling occurs when societies become more similar through diffusion.

Example: US fast food culture is widely diffused globally.

🎓 Theoretical Perspectives on Culture
Functionalism (Macro) Culture helps people meet their biological, instrumental, and expressive needs. Society and culture work together symbiotically — each reinforcing the other. Culture serves vital functions for social stability.
Conflict Theory (Macro) Cultural ideas are creations of the most powerful members of society and can be used by the ruling class to influence the thoughts and actions of other classes. Culture can be a tool of control — "cultural occupation" of the lower class.
Symbolic Interactionism (Micro) People create, maintain, and modify culture during their everyday activities. However, cultural creations can take on a life of their own and end up controlling people — what we create can end up shaping us.
Postmodernism (Micro) Much of culture today is based on simulation of reality rather than reality itself. Television and the internet present curated, constructed versions of "reality" that people increasingly accept as authentic.
🏛️ High Culture vs. Popular Culture
High CultureCultural products associated with elite groups — classical music, opera, fine art, literary fiction. Typically associated with high social status and education.
Popular CultureCultural products and practices widely consumed by the general public — pop music, television, social media, fast food, sports.
The distinction between high and popular culture is increasingly blurred in the modern era — what was once considered "low" culture is now studied seriously by sociologists.
🌐 The Global Village — Marshall McLuhan (1960s)
McLuhan popularised the term "global village" — the idea that the world is being 'shrunk' through immediate electronic communications. Time and space differences are becoming irrelevant as technology connects the world.
  • Enables rapid cultural diffusion and levelling across societies.
  • Raises questions: Is technology genuinely bringing people closer together, or simply creating the illusion of connection?
  • Cultural levelling may reduce diversity — dominant cultures spread; minority cultures erode.

What Is Sociological Theory?
🧠 Definition & Overview
A sociological theory is a set of ideas that provides a framework for understanding how society works — why it is the way it is, how it changes, and who benefits or suffers from its arrangements.

Macro Theories

Functionalism and Conflict Theory operate at the macro level — they examine large-scale social structures, institutions, and forces that shape society as a whole.

Micro Theory

Symbolic Interactionism operates at the micro level — it studies how individuals create meaning through daily interaction and how these build up into social reality.

Multi-Paradigm

Max Weber bridges all three perspectives — showing that the most powerful sociological insights draw from multiple theoretical traditions simultaneously.

📊 How the Four Perspectives Relate
No single paradigm tells the whole story — together, they give a complete picture of social life.

Part 01 — Functionalism · Macro Perspective
⚙️ What Is Functionalism?
Functionalism views society as a system of interrelated parts working together to maintain stability and order. It is a macro-level perspective that examines how social structures affect the way society functions as a whole.
  • Institutions like family, economy, education, and politics are critical for society to function.
  • A society's values and norms provide the foundation for rules, laws, and institutional relationships.
  • Things occur in society for a specific function — and those functions help maintain overall stability.
  • Society is analogous to a living organism — every part serves a purpose.
  • Change is slow and disruptive; stability is the default and desired state.
  • Social problems are seen as malfunctions of the system — temporary disorders.
👥 Five Key Functionalist Theorists

01 · 1798–1857

Auguste Comte

Founder of Sociology

Argued sociology should discover social laws — unchanging statements of fact. Introduced Social Statics (stable structures) and Social Dynamics (processes of change).

02 · 1820–1903

Herbert Spencer

Society as a Living Organism

Applied Darwin's natural selection to societies. Argued that stronger societies survive, weaker ones become extinct — Social Darwinism. Heavily criticised for justifying inequality and colonial exploitation.

03 · 1858–1917

Émile Durkheim

Pioneer of Data-Driven Sociology

Solidarity is the vital force holding society together. Introduced Mechanical (traditional) vs. Organic (modern, industrial) solidarity. Famous for studying suicide as a social fact.

04 · Talcott Parsons

Talcott Parsons

Social Inertia & Bicycle Wheel

Saw society like a bicycle wheel — one broken spoke disrupts everything. Social inertia means change requires a powerful external force to overcome the system's momentum toward stability.

05 · 1910–2003

Robert Merton

Middle-Range Theory & Functions

Broke sociology into parts instead of grand theories. Introduced Manifest Functions (intended, obvious) and Latent Functions (unintended, hidden). Asked "For whom is this functional?"

🔗 Durkheim — Two Types of Solidarity
Mechanical SolidarityOrganic Solidarity
Found in traditional, pre-industrial societies Found in modern, industrial societies
People bonded by shared beliefs, values, and common activities — the collective overwhelms the individual A diverse division of labour forces people to depend on one another — interdependence creates cohesion
Example: small, homogeneous rural villages Example: complex urban economies
📉 Durkheim — 4 Types of Suicide
Suicide rates are social facts shaped by solidarity and social control — not just individual psychology.
EgoisticIndividual lacks meaningful bonds — isolation leads to purposelessness. Low social integration. E.g. social isolation, detachment from community.
AltruisticIdentity completely absorbed by the group — self-sacrifice seen as a duty. E.g. soldiers, extreme ideological groups.
FatalisticHigh social control — excessive regulation leaves no room for individual autonomy. Life feels inescapably constrained. E.g. enslaved persons, authoritarian regimes.
AnomicLow social control — sudden collapse of norms following rapid change removes moral guidance. E.g. economic crises, sudden wealth, social upheaval.
🔭 Merton — Manifest vs. Latent Functions

Manifest Functions

Intended, expected consequences of a social event or institution — the obvious, openly stated purposes.

🔍 Example: Schools educate students → intended function: produce a literate, skilled workforce.

Latent Functions

Unintended, unforeseen consequences — the hidden effects that may not be immediately obvious.

🔍 Example: Schools also create peer networks and reinforce social class hierarchies → unintended consequences.

💡 Applied Example (Buying a Cadillac):
Manifest Motives (freely admitted): "It's comfortable", "It performs well", "High quality car".
Latent Motives (unconscious/unstated): "It shows I'm successful", "It makes me feel powerful", "It signals status to others".
Merton's guiding question: "For whom is this functional?"
⚠️ Ignores Power & Wealth

Functionalism fails to account for the influence of wealth and power on how society is shaped. Who decides what is "functional"?

⚠️ Supports the Status Quo

Accused of defending existing social arrangements — even harmful ones — by framing them as "necessary" for stability.

⚠️ Minorities Are Overlooked

The view that society works "for the greatest number" risks ignoring minority groups who may be harmed by current structures.

⚠️ Underestimates Inequality

Fails to recognise how class, race, and gender inequalities perpetuate systematic imbalance within society.

⚠️ Change Is Undervalued

By emphasising stability, functionalism downplays social change and conflict as engines of progress and justice.

⚠️ Too Abstract

Grand functionalist theories (especially Parsons) are often criticised for being too abstract and difficult to test empirically with real-world data.

Part 02 — Conflict Theory · Macro Perspective
⚡ What Is Conflict Theory?
A theoretical framework that views society as an ongoing struggle for scarce resources — where wealth and power determine who wins.
Power PyramidSociety has a pyramid structure of power and wealth. The elite at the top set the rules for everyone below.
Scarce ResourcesEveryone competes for limited "stuff" — money, power, status, or rights. Conflict is built into the system.
Broad ApplicationApplies to social class, race, gender, marriage, religion, criminal justice, environment, and international relations.
Class ConsciousnessWorkers and oppressed groups must recognise their shared position before they can organise for change. Without awareness, the status quo continues unchallenged.
📌 How Conflict Theorists See Society
  • Economic Wealth & Power — The two central concerns. Control over material resources determines who controls law, education, media, and culture.
  • Discrimination Is Structural — Ageism, sexism, racism, and classism persist because those in power promote their interests over others.
  • Universal Scope — From a marriage to a multinational corporation, conflict theory reveals power dynamics at every scale — local, national, and global.
  • Social Activism — Conflict theory calls for recognition of structural inequality and systemic change.
👥 Five Key Conflict Theorists

01 · 1818–1883

Karl Marx

Class Conflict & Capitalism

"The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles."
BourgeoisieOwn the means of production. Control businesses workers depend on for survival.
ProletariatDo all the labour but reap few of the benefits. Will never get ahead if they do not share in the wealth they create.
False ConsciousnessWorkers fail to change fate because they lack understanding of their true position in the capitalist system.

Solution: Develop class consciousness, unite, replace private ownership with socialised property.

02 · 1802–1876

Harriet Martineau

Pioneer Female Sociologist — Gender, Slavery & Inequality

  • One of the first female sociologists. Extended conflict theory beyond class to encompass gender.
  • Analysed the impact of slavery on American society — documented the daily reality of enslaved people.
  • Examined the subordinate position of women in political, economic, and social life.
  • Key Insight: Economic inequality and gender inequality are connected — you cannot understand one without the other.

03 · 1868–1963

W.E.B. Du Bois

Race, Racism & the African American Experience

"The problem of the twentieth century is the problem of the color-line."
Philadelphia NegroFirst major sociological study of an African American community. Proved poverty was primarily a result of prejudice and discrimination.
Double ConsciousnessThe lived experience of navigating two worlds simultaneously — a white world while maintaining a separate Black identity.

Co-founded the NAACP. Eventually concluded racial equality within the US was unlikely without radical structural change.

04 · 1860–1935

Jane Addams

Poverty, Social Work & Hull House — Nobel Peace Prize 1931

Poverty results from ignorance and structural barriers, not moral failing. Co-founded Hull House (Chicago, 1889) — model for social reform. Three founding principles:

  • Live Among the Poor — understand problems firsthand, not from a comfortable distance.
  • Universal Human Dignity — every person has worth regardless of race, ethnicity, gender, or class.
  • Education Overcomes Poverty — service and education overcome structural forces that perpetuate poverty.

05 · 1953–

John Bellamy Foster

Capitalism, Ecology & the Limits of Free Markets

"Purely capitalist economies — markets that are totally free — are disappearing throughout the world."
  • Extends Marx's critique of capitalism into the 21st century — adding ecology to the conflict framework.
  • Markets Cannot Solve Everything — unchecked capitalism inflicts damage on both society and the natural environment.
  • The post-WWII US economic expansion was driven by rebuilding war-devastated nations — not by the natural superiority of free markets.
⚠️ Ignores Social Stability

Conflict theory is criticised for focusing so heavily on struggle and competition that it ignores the cooperative aspects of social life that actually hold society together.

⚠️ Overly Economic

Marx in particular is accused of reducing all social conflict to economics — ignoring the role of culture, values, and identity in shaping inequality.

⚠️ Status Quo vs Change

By constantly emphasising conflict, the theory may overlook situations where existing structures actually benefit the majority or have evolved fairly.

Part 03 — Symbolic Interactionism · Micro Perspective
🔤 What Is Symbolic Interactionism?
Symbolic Interactionism focuses on how symbols and communication influence the way people's interactions with each other create the social world in which we live.
Symbols Are ArbitrarySymbols have no inherent meaning — their meaning is socially constructed and assigned through shared agreement.
Shared DefinitionsCreate social order — when people share the same meanings, coordinated behaviour becomes possible.
Core FocusMicro-level daily interactions — the face-to-face encounters through which meaning is created and society is built from the bottom up.
Key InsightSymbols help us define a situation, determine what we should do — and help others understand who we are.
👥 Four Key Symbolic Interactionist Theorists

01 · 1863–1931

George Herbert Mead

Mind, Self & Society · Founder of SI

The self is not fixed — it is fluid, changing as we encounter new symbols and interactions.

1
Mind

Symbols enter our minds and are interpreted — telling us how to react.

2
Self

Through interpreting symbols, we develop an identity that separates us from others. Couldn't have a self without symbols or without others to pass them to you.

3
Society

We take our identity into the world and interact with other identities — collectively creating society.

02 · 1900–1987

Herbert Blumer

Coined "Symbolic Interactionism" · Three Core Premises

Blumer formalised SI by establishing three foundational premises that define the entire perspective.

1
Meaning Drives Behaviour

"Human beings behave toward things on the basis of the meanings they ascribe to those things." — It is not the thing itself, but what it means to us that matters.

2
Meaning Is Social

"Meaning arises out of the social interaction one has with others." — Not intrinsic to objects; socially constructed through interaction.

3
Meaning Is Modified

Meanings are "handled and modified through an interpretive process." — We actively interpret and sometimes revise them as we navigate new situations.

03 · 1922–1982

Erving Goffman

Dramaturgy · The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life (1959)

All life is like acting. Daily social interactions are like the gestures of actors on a stage — and we are all performers.
Front StageThe public performance — how we present ourselves to others. We use props (clothes, objects, settings) to manage our image.
Back StageThe private space where the performance relaxes. We drop our social mask and behave differently when the "audience" isn't watching.
Impression MgmtThe active effort to control what others think of us. Every person is both a performer and an audience simultaneously.

04 · 1928–2023

Howard Becker

Labelling Theory · Outsiders (1963)

Deviance is not a quality of the act a person commits, but rather a consequence of the application by others of rules and sanctions.
Labels Are PowerThe power to label is the power to define reality. Groups with high social status determine which behaviours are "deviant".
The OutsiderThose labelled deviant become "outsiders". The label can become a master status — overriding all other aspects of identity.
Self-Fulfilling ProphecyOnce labelled, individuals may internalise the identity and act accordingly — the label itself shapes future behaviour.
⚠️ Neglects Social Structure

SI is so focused on individual interactions that it neglects the larger structural context (class, power, capitalism, law, policy) that shapes the very interactions it studies.

⚠️ Over-empowers the Individual

By emphasising individual meaning-making, SI overstates human agency and understates the constraints that social structures place on individual behaviour.

⚠️ Risk of Micro-Bias

Its micro focus means SI may miss the big picture — large-scale social forces (capitalism, law, policy) that shape the individual actions it studies.

Part 04 — Max Weber · The Multi-Paradigm Sociologist
🌐 Who Was Max Weber?
Max Weber is a special sociologist because he cannot be labelled under just one theory. His ideas span conflict theory, functionalism, and symbolic interactionism simultaneously — making him one of the most versatile and debated thinkers in the discipline.

Response to Marx

Accepted that social class influences outcomes — but argued Marx's two-class model was far too simple. All people experience economic, political, and cultural conflicts tied to their social position.

Ideas Matter

Weber showed how values, beliefs, and ideas — not just material forces — are powerful shapers of social behaviour and even economic systems.

Multi-Dimensional Inequality

Proposed three dimensions of social stratification: Class (economic), Status (social honour), and Party (political power) — a far richer model than Marx's class-only view.

💰 Class (Economic Position)

A person's relationship to the means of production — what you earn, what you own, and what access you have to economic resources.

👑 Status (Social Honour)

Social prestige — how much respect and honour people receive regardless of their economic wealth. A judge may have high status without being rich.

🗳️ Party (Political Power)

Organised efforts to gain power over others. Political influence, associations, and organisations that shape collective decisions and resource distribution.

🏢 Weber as Functionalist — The Ideal Bureaucracy
Weber argued that rational bureaucracies naturally emerge in complex modern societies. He identified the characteristics of the ideal-type bureaucracy and its functions.
  • Clear lines of authority and hierarchy
  • Division of labour allowing workers to specialise
  • Formal, written rules and defined expectations
  • Impersonal relationships based on role, not person
  • Predictable, consistent outcomes — regardless of who holds any given role
Bureaucracy is the most efficient form of social organisation for large, complex modern institutions. But it also has an "iron cage" effect — trapping people in impersonal systems.
👑 Weber's 3 Types of Authority
Traditional AuthorityBased on long-standing customs, traditions, and the belief that "this is how it has always been done." E.g. monarchies, inherited leadership.
Charismatic AuthorityBased on the exceptional personal qualities of a leader — people follow because of who the leader is. E.g. religious prophets, revolutionary leaders.
Legal-Rational AuthorityBased on rules, laws, and procedures — authority resides in the office, not the person. The foundation of modern bureaucratic states and organisations.
💡 Weber argued that modern societies tend toward legal-rational authority — charismatic leadership is unstable and traditional authority is eroded by modernisation.
✝️ Weber as Symbolic Interactionist — The Protestant Ethic & the Spirit of Capitalism (1905)
Weber showed that values influence social structure — he argued that ideas, not just material forces, are powerful shapers of behaviour. This directly anticipates symbolic interactionism: if meaning shapes behaviour, then whoever controls the dominant meanings controls behaviour.
1
Calvinist Theology

Calvinists believed they were predestined to heaven or hell — but looked for signs of God's favour in worldly success.

2
Protestant Work Ethic

Hard work, discipline, frugality, and rejection of worldly pleasures became religious virtues — not just practical habits.

3
Capital Accumulation

Because spending on pleasure was frowned upon, profits were reinvested — creating the capital accumulation that fuels capitalism.

4
Spirit of Capitalism

A cultural ethos valuing disciplined, rational pursuit of profit emerged — shaped by Protestant values, not just economic necessity.

💡 Weber's argument: Capitalism did not arise purely from economic forces — it emerged from the specific cultural meaning that Protestantism gave to work and wealth. Ideas shape economies.
⚖️ Value-Free Sociology
Weber argued that sociology must be value-free — separating the researcher's personal values from their scientific findings.
  • Researchers should put personal, political, and cultural biases aside when conducting research.
  • Acknowledge that values may shape which topics they study — but the research itself must be objective.
  • Facts and values must be kept separate — sociology should explain the world, not prescribe how it should be.
⚠️ Weber's Own Paradox: Ironically, the choice of what to study is itself value-laden. Many sociologists argue true value-freedom is impossible — but striving toward it promotes credibility and rigour.
🧠 Verstehen — Interpretive Understanding
Verstehen (German for "understanding") — Weber's method of understanding social behaviour by empathetically entering into the subjective experience of social actors.

To explain social action, you must understand what it means to the person doing it — not just observe it externally. This is why sociology requires interpretive methods, not just statistical analysis.

  • Goes beyond observation to ask: "What does this action mean to the actor?"
  • Bridges the gap between natural science (explanation) and social science (interpretation).
  • Directly anticipates symbolic interactionism — meaning shapes behaviour.

Part 01 — Ethics & Morality: Definitions & Branches
📖 Ethics vs. Morality — Key Distinction
The terms ethics and morality are often used interchangeably — and in casual conversation, there is no problem switching between them. However, there is a distinction in philosophy.
EtymologyBoth words share the same roots: Latin mores (manner and customs) and Greek ethos (custom and habits).
MoralityA first-order set of beliefs and practices about how to live a good life — the standards and conduct we actually hold and practise.
EthicsThe formal, second-order study of those moral standards and conduct — a conscious, systematic reflection on the adequacy of our moral beliefs. Also called "moral philosophy."
Key DifferenceMorality = what we believe is right/wrong. Ethics = the study and analysis of why those beliefs are or aren't justified.
⚖️ Ethics, Law & Morality — The Venn Model
Ethics, law, and morality are related but distinct — they overlap in important ways but are not identical.
EthicsFormal study of moral standards — systematic and reflective. Can demand more than law requires.
LawCodified rules with enforceable sanctions. The minimum floor for behaviour — not the ceiling.
MoralityPersonal or cultural beliefs about right and wrong. May differ from what is legally required.
⚖️ Key principle: "Ethics is knowing the difference between what you have a right to do and what is right to do." — Justice Potter Stewart
Law is the ethical minimum — ethics demands more.
🌿 The Four Branches of Ethics

01 — Descriptive Ethics

The factual study of the ethical standards or principles of a group or tradition. What do people actually believe? An empirical, not normative, discipline.

02 — Normative Ethics

The development of theories that systematically define right and wrong actions. What should people do? Includes Utilitarianism, Deontology, and Virtue Ethics.

03 — Applied Ethics

Using normative theories to form judgments regarding practical cases — bioethics, business ethics, environmental ethics, medical ethics, IT ethics.

04 — Meta-Ethics

Careful analysis of the meaning and justification of ethical claims. What does it mean to say something is "good"? Are moral statements true or false?

Part 02 — Eight Moral Theories: On What Basis Do We Decide?
🕊️ 1. Supernaturalism

God-based ethics. Morality is grounded in divine command — something is good because God says so. The only source of moral rules is God. Do what the Bible tells you or follow the will of God.

💡 2. Intuitionism

Goodness doesn't need proving — moral truth is self-evident to reflective adults who can intuitively detect it through careful reflection. We simply know right from wrong when we think carefully.

📊 3. Consequentialism (Utilitarianism)

Actions are right or wrong based on their consequences. Choose the alternative that produces the greatest balance of good over evil for the greatest number. Morality is judged by outcomes, not intentions.

⚖️ 4. Duty Ethics (Deontology)

Some acts are inherently right or wrong regardless of consequences. Rules and duties matter absolutely. Kant's Categorical Imperative: "Act so that the maxim may become a universal law for all rational beings."

🌟 5. Virtue Ethics

Focus on good character traits — virtues. What would a virtuous person do in this circumstance? Aristotle's "Golden Mean" between excess and deficiency. The central question is not "What should I do?" but "What kind of person should I be?"

🛡️ 6. Situation Ethics

Reject rigid rules — decisions are based on the unique situation, seeking the best for those involved. Context is everything. A good person would do what the circumstances call for — not blindly follow rules.

🔒 7. Rights Ethics

All people possess certain inalienable rights — the most influential moral idea of the past two centuries. Natural rights: life, health, liberty, and possession. Any act that violates an individual's rights is ethically unacceptable regardless of social benefit.

🧬 8. Evolutionary Ethics

Moral behaviour is that which tends to aid in human survival — grounded in Darwin's evolutionary biology. Human social behaviour is an extended development of biological evolution. Problem: What counts as "progress"?

💼 Ethical Egoism

The only person you have a moral obligation to look after is yourself. Ayn Rand's Objectivism: "Man's life — the life proper to a rational being — is the standard of moral values." Altruism is incompatible with man's nature.

📊 Utilitarianism — Four Key Thinkers
The right action is whatever produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number of people. Morality is judged by consequences, not intentions.
Epicurus (341–270 BC)Hedonistic utilitarianism — seeks to reduce suffering and increase pleasure. "We count pleasure as the originating principle and the goal for the blessed life."
Hutcheson (1694–1747)"The action is best, which procures the greatest happiness for the greatest number; and that worst, which occasions misery."
Bentham (1748–1832)Bentham's Utilitarian Calculus — a method for measuring the quantity of pleasure or pain produced by an action to determine its moral worth.
Mill (1806–1873)"Actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote general happiness; wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of general happiness."
🌟 Virtue Ethics — Plato & Aristotle
Morality is a matter of being a good person, which involves having virtuous character traits.
Plato (427–347 BCE)Identified the four cardinal virtues: wisdom, courage, temperance, and justice. Accepted even by early Christian theologians.
Aristotle (384–322 BCE)Nicomachean Ethics — Moral development requires practice and habit. Virtue is the "Golden Mean" between excess and deficiency.
💡 Central question: Not "What should I do?" but "What kind of person should I be?" — Virtue ethics focuses on character over rules.
⚖️ Kant's Categorical Imperative
"Act so that the maxim [determining motive of the will] may be capable of becoming a universal law for all rational beings."
Reason-BasedHuman reason — not feeling, tradition, or consequences — makes moral demands on our lives. Morality is universal and rational.
Universal Law TestBefore acting, ask: could this action become a universal law for all rational beings? If the maxim cannot be universalised, the action is morally wrong.
Respect for PersonsWe have a moral responsibility to treat people always as ends in themselves — never merely as means to our own goals.
🧠 Moral Reason vs. Moral Feeling
Reason (Samuel Clarke, 1675–1729)Feeling (David Hume, 1711–1776)
Morality is strictly a matter of rational judgment. Since Plato, moral truths are thought to exist in a spiritual realm — like mathematical truths, they are eternal. Morality is strictly a matter of feeling (emotion). "Fashion, vogue, custom, and law are the chief foundation of all moral determinations." We have a moral sense — an emotional intuition.
💡 Modern synthesis: Most contemporary ethicists combine both — reason shapes our ethical thinking, but feeling motivates moral action. Neither alone is sufficient.
🔑 Three Key Ethical Principles for Professionals
1. Imperative PrincipleDo what is right — act according to absolute moral rules. An act is good or bad in and of itself. "Do unto others what you want others to do unto you."
2. Utilitarian PrincipleDo what produces the greatest good. An act is good if it produces good outcomes for the greatest number. "The greatest good for the greatest number of people."
3. Generalisation ArgumentIf the result of everyone doing a certain action is undesirable, no one has a right to do that action. A combination of imperative and utilitarian principles. "If it were desirable for everyone to do X, then one has the right to do X."
Part 03 — Ethical Relativism
🌍 Classical Ethical Relativism — The Core Claim
Classical ethical/cultural relativism holds that moral standards differ from one society to another, and that there is no independent universal standard by which cultures can be judged.
Xenophanes (570–475 BCE)"Ethiopians say their gods are flat-nosed and dark, Thracians that theirs are blue-eyed and red-haired." Each culture projects its own form onto the divine.
Herodotus (484–425 BCE)"Everyone without exception believes his own native customs, and the religion he was brought up in, to be the best." First documented vast moral diversity across cultures.
Sextus Empiricus (200 CE)Documented moral standards that differ between societies: homosexuality, cannibalism, infanticide, theft. Concluded we should doubt the existence of an independent universal morality.
Montaigne / Hume"Custom has the power to shape every possible kind of cultural practice." "Fashion, vogue, custom, and law are the chief foundation of all moral determinations."
❓ The core question: Does the fact that moral standards vary across cultures mean that there are no universal moral truths? Ethical relativism says yes — but this view is itself debated.

Unintentionally Amoral

  • "If it's not illegal, it's allowed" mentality
  • Moral gears stuck in neutral — no "ethics radar"

THE "GOOD GUYS" · ETHICAL EXCELLENCE

  • Ethical leadership is central to their approach
  • Follows both the letter and the spirit of the law

Habits of Moral Leaders

Part 06 — Moral Development & Moral Judgment
📈 Kohlberg's Stages of Moral Development
How ethical thinking matures over a lifetime — progressing from self-interest to principled conscience.
1
Pre-Conventional: Punishment Avoidance

Stage 1: Avoid punishment. Stage 2: Act in one's own self-interest.

3
Conventional: Social Approval / Law & Order

Stage 3: Do what gains approval — please others ("Good Boy/Girl"). Stage 4: Follow rules, fulfil duties, respect authority.

5
Post-Conventional: Social Contract & Principled Conscience

Stage 5: Genuine interest in welfare of others — social contract. Stage 6: Respect for universal ethical principles.

📊 Most managers settle at Stage 3–4 (seeking approval and following rules). Moral managers aspire to Stage 5–6: guided by principle and conscience.
💜 Gilligan's Ethics of Care
Gilligan challenged Kohlberg's model as male-biased. Women's moral reasoning emphasises care, relationships, and context rather than abstract rules and justice.
1
Self as Sole Object

The individual considers only their own needs and interests. Self-preservation is the primary driver.

2
Connections & Social Life

Recognising and participating in social relationships. Decisions account for others through active connection.

3
Self and Others Equally

Recognising and responding to both personal needs and those of others with equal moral weight and care.

👁️ 1. Moral Imagination

The ability to see ethical dimensions in situations that may not obviously present them — recognising that an ethical issue exists in the first place.

🔍 2. Moral Identification & Ordering

Recognising and ordering the relevant ethical issues and stakeholders at play — who is affected and how much weight each concern deserves.

⚖️ 3. Moral Evaluation

Applying ethical frameworks to determine the right, fair, and just course of action from among competing alternatives.

🌊 4. Tolerance of Ambiguity

Accepting that ethical dilemmas are genuinely difficult — resisting easy shortcuts and comfortable rationalisations when the right answer is unclear.

🔗 5. Integrated Competence

Weaving ethical thinking into managerial decision-making as a matter of course — not as a separate step, but as part of how decisions are made every day.

💎 6. Sense of Obligation

A personal commitment to doing right independent of external incentives — doing the right thing because it is right, not because someone is watching.

🔀 Three Types of Moral Dilemma
A moral dilemma arises when you have moral reasons to do each of two actions — but doing both is not possible. They stem from vagueness, conflicting reasons, or genuine disagreement.

Epistemic

Two or more moral requirements exist but it is unclear which takes precedence. You are unsure which duty wins.

Ontological

Two genuine moral requirements both apply and neither is overridden. You cannot fulfil both — a genuine moral tragedy.

Self-Imposed vs World-Imposed

Self-imposed: created by your own prior actions (e.g., conflicting promises). World-imposed: external circumstances create the conflict.

Part 02 — What is Whistleblowing?
📢 Three Definitions of Whistleblowing
GeneralCalling public attention to wrongdoing — typically to avert harm — by a member or former member of an organisation.
Legal (Malaysia SIA)Disclosure of information reasonably believed to evidence a contravention of any law, regulation, mismanagement, corruption, or abuse of authority.
UK Public Life"Raising a concern about wrongdoing within an organisation or through an independent structure associated with it."
⚠️ Social reality: Whistleblowers are rarely honoured by peers — often perceived as disloyal or a "traitor" to the organisation. This social and professional risk is a major barrier to disclosure.
🔀 Three Types of Whistleblowing

Internal Whistleblowing

Disclosure made to someone within the organisation — a manager, ethics officer, or board. First line of action; preserves confidentiality. Least disruptive.

Personal Whistleblowing

Targeted at a specific individual — the charge is against a person, not the organisation or its systems.

External Whistleblowing

Disclosure to a party outside the organisation — regulator, media, or law enforcement. Highest personal risk; most public impact.

🎯 Purpose & Characteristics of Whistleblowers

What Whistleblowing Achieves

  • Draws attention to unethical, inappropriate, or incompetent conduct
  • Protects those harmed — or at risk of harm — by institutional actions
  • Acts where others are unaware of the risk or refuse to acknowledge it

What Defines a Whistleblower

  • Takes full personal responsibility for their actions
  • Concern for ethical values overrides personal self-preservation
  • Risks financial security, counter-accusation, and alienation — yet feels compelled to act
Part 03 — When is Whistleblowing Justified? De George's 5-Condition Framework
⚖️ De George's Five-Condition Framework
De George distinguishes between when whistleblowing is morally permissible (conditions 1–3 met) and when it becomes morally obligatory (conditions 4–5 also met). Meeting all 5 means you are not only allowed — you are ethically required to act.

✅ Morally Permissible — Conditions 1–3

1
Serious & Considerable Harm

The firm's product or policy causes serious and considerable harm to the public — not minor inconvenience or personal grievance.

2
Internal Reporting First

The employee has reported the concern to their immediate superior and made their moral concern known clearly to management.

3
Internal Channels Exhausted

Internal procedures have been fully exhausted — including escalating up the management chain to the board if necessary — without result.

⚠️ Morally Obligatory — + Conditions 4–5

4
Documented Evidence

The whistleblower has documented evidence that would convince a reasonable, impartial observer of the danger. Not a feeling — verifiable, documented proof.

5
Good Reason for Effective Change

There is good reason to believe that going public will bring about the necessary changes — the risk of disclosure is worth the outcome. Futile disclosure is not obligatory.

Part 04 — The 8 Stages of a Whistleblowing Incident
🔍 Stages 1–4: Recognising & Assessing the Problem
1
Is There a Potential WB Scenario?

Three triggers: (1) you believe your organisation is acting unethically, (2) you hold relevant inside knowledge, and (3) you believe disclosure would change the course of action.

2
Seriousness Test

Ask: is this issue serious enough to warrant concern? Most scenarios that pass Stage 1 fail here. Do not escalate trivial matters — the threshold must be genuine and significant.

3
Reality Check

Pause. Gather more information. Discuss with trusted colleagues. Ensure you can rationally explain why you reached a different conclusion from your organisation's position.

4
The Big Picture

View the problem beyond your own perspective — consider technical, financial, legal, and strategic dimensions. Only once fully convinced, proceed to action.

📣 Stages 5–8: Escalating & Living with the Outcome
5
Force Management Recognition

Start internally — make middle management aware of the issue. Begin accumulating documents. Consider professional society support and seek legal advice early.

6
Take It to Upper Management

Going over your boss's head puts your job at risk. Anonymous escalation is possible but taken less seriously. You are now at the threshold of full whistleblowing.

7
Go Outside the Organisation

Regulatory agencies and legal authorities are often better than the media — they can impose fines and compel action immediately, without the sensationalism of press exposure.

8
Living with the Results

Expect a career change: transfer to a dead end, or termination. Ensure financial security before disclosure. Professional associations can provide moral and practical support.

💡 Key insight: Whistleblowing is a process, not a single act. Preparation and documentation at every stage are critical to both effectiveness and personal protection.

Benefits

❌ Immoral Management

THE "BAD GUYS" · ACTIVELY OPPOSES ETHICS

  • Intentionally does wrong — not through ignorance
  • Actively opposes what is right, fair, or just
  • Exploits others; treats law as an obstacle to overcome
  • Focused solely on personal or organisational profit
  • Zero concern for stakeholders
😐 Amoral Management

THE MOST COMMON · ETHICAL BLIND SPOT

Intentionally Amoral

  • Believes ethics and business should not mix
  • Deliberately separates ethics from decisions
  • "What can we get away with?" mentality

Unintentionally Amoral

  • Simply doesn't "think ethically" in decisions
  • "If it's not illegal, it's allowed" mentality
  • Moral gears stuck in neutral — no "ethics radar"

✅ Moral Management

THE "GOOD GUYS" · ETHICAL EXCELLENCE

  • Conforms to the highest standards of ethical conduct
  • Ethical leadership is central to their approach
  • Follows both the letter and the spirit of the law
  • Operates well above what laws require
  • Considers all stakeholders proactively
  • Possesses acute moral sense and moral maturity

⚠️ Recognise the Risk

🍩 Doughnut Economics — Kate Raworth (2017)
A framework that redefines what it means for an economy to thrive — balancing human needs within planetary boundaries. The goal: live in the "safe and just space" between the inner and outer rings.

🔴 The Inner Ring — Social Foundation

Minimum standard every person deserves — if we fall short in any of these, we are in the "social shortfall."

Food & Water Health Education Income & Work Peace & Justice Political Voice Gender Equality Housing & Energy

🟢 The Doughnut — Safe & Just Space

Between the two rings lies the goal: an economy that meets all human needs without overshooting Earth's ecological ceiling. New Zealand, Iceland & Scotland use Wellbeing Budgets aligned with this model.

🔵 The Outer Ring — Ecological Ceiling

Upper limit of resource use — if we breach these, we enter the "ecological overshoot."

Climate Change Ocean Acidification Chemical Pollution Freshwater Use Land Conversion Biodiversity Loss Ozone Depletion
💡 Relevance to Development Economics:
· Beyond GDP: Challenges GDP as the sole measure — wellbeing, equality & ecological health must be tracked.
· Redefines Poverty: Social foundation defines poverty as falling short in any dimension — food, health, education, voice — not just income below a dollar threshold.
· Sustainable Growth: Inflation-driven growth that erodes disposable income OR exceeds planetary limits is harmful in both purchasing power AND ecological terms.
🔗 How the Economic Concepts Interlink
1
Higher GDP → Higher Per Capita Income → More Disposable Income

Economic growth raises average living standards — but only if the gains are distributed equitably across income groups.

2
High Inflation Erodes Disposable Income → Pushes More Into Poverty

Even if wages grow, if inflation outpaces wage growth, purchasing power falls — hitting the poorest hardest.

3
Economic Growth Without Equity Keeps Poverty Entrenched

Per capita income masks inequality. Trickle-down policies repeatedly fail the poorest quintile — the Doughnut Economics approach demands distributional justice as a core goal.

4
GNP Tracks Nationals' Contribution; GDP Measures Domestic Output

For small open economies with large diaspora (e.g., Sri Lanka), GNP may differ significantly from GDP due to remittances.

Part 01 — Consent in Contract Law: 5 Elements of Valid Contractual Consent
📝 Five Elements of Valid Contractual Consent
For a contract to be legally binding, all five elements must be present. Any missing element — or a vitiating factor — can void or render the contract voidable.
1
Offer & Acceptance

One party makes a clear offer; the other accepts unconditionally. The acceptance must mirror the offer exactly — the "mirror image rule." A counter-offer kills the original offer.

2
Intention to Create Legal Relations

Both parties must intend to be legally bound. Social/domestic agreements are presumed NOT legally binding. Commercial agreements ARE presumed legally binding.

3
Consideration

Each party must give something of value — there must be a bargain. Consent alone is not enough. A promise without consideration is not enforceable (past consideration is generally insufficient).

4
Capacity

Parties must have legal capacity — adults of sound mind. Contracts with minors, intoxicated, or mentally incapacitated persons are voidable (can be set aside at the vulnerable party's option).

5
Genuine Consent

Consent must be real and not obtained by misrepresentation, mistake, duress, or undue influence. Flawed consent can void or render a contract voidable.

⚠️ Vitiating Factors — these invalidate apparent consent:
Misrepresentation · Common Mistake · Duress · Undue Influence · Unconscionable Bargain
ℹ️ Informed Consent — What Must Be Disclosed
Informed consent requires that a person is given sufficient, clear, and comprehensible information about what they are agreeing to — before they agree. Central to medical law, data protection, and research ethics.
  • The nature and purpose of the proposed action
  • Material risks and likely consequences
  • Available alternatives and their risks
  • The right to refuse or withdraw without penalty
  • Who will have access to any information provided
🏥 Informed Consent in Context
Medical ContextSurgeon must explain the procedure, risks, alternatives, and prognosis (Montgomery v Lanarkshire [2015]). Patient must understand before signing.
GDPR / DataConsent must be specific, informed, and unambiguous. Pre-ticked boxes are NOT valid. Purpose must be clearly stated. Who, What, Why, How must all be disclosed.
ResearchParticipants can withdraw from research at any time without consequence. Anonymised data already collected may generally be retained.
Part 02 — Vitiating Factors: Duress, Undue Influence, Misrepresentation & Mistake
⚡ Duress — Three Types
Consent obtained under duress is voidable. The threat must be illegitimate — a lawful threat (e.g., "I will sue you") may NOT constitute duress.
Actual ViolenceDirect physical threat to a person or their family. Renders contract voidable. The most clear-cut form of duress.
Economic DuressIllegitimate commercial pressure that leaves no practical choice — e.g., threatening to breach a contract unless new terms are accepted (Atlas Express v Kafco [1989]).
Duress to GoodsThreat to damage or withhold property. Courts have accepted this as grounds to vitiate consent, though economic duress has largely superseded it in modern law.
🔗 Undue Influence — Three Types
Actual UIProved by direct evidence — one party actively pressured the other into the agreement. Must be demonstrated on the facts.
Presumed (Class 1)Certain relationships automatically presumed: solicitor/client, doctor/patient, parent/child, religious leader/follower — a presumption arises that influence was exercised.
Presumed (Class 2)Some relationships raise a presumption only on the facts — trust & confidence in the other party (e.g., husband/wife in some cases: Barclays Bank v O'Brien).
🚫 No Penalty for Refusing

A person must be able to say "no" without suffering negative consequences — loss of a service, employment, or benefit. If refusal carries a penalty, the consent of those who agree is also suspect.

📦 No Bundling of Consent

Consent cannot be bundled into a single agreement covering multiple unrelated purposes. Each distinct purpose requires its own specific consent — particularly critical under GDPR data processing rules.

⚖️ Power Imbalance

When one party holds significantly more power (employer/employee, authority/citizen), courts scrutinise whether consent was truly voluntary. The vulnerable party may have felt unable to refuse.

🎭 Misrepresentation — Three Types
FraudulentA false statement made knowingly, without belief in its truth, or recklessly. Gives right to rescission AND damages in tort (Derry v Peek [1889]).
NegligentA false statement made carelessly without reasonable grounds for believing it true. Damages available under Misrepresentation Act 1967 s.2(1).
InnocentA false statement made honestly with reasonable belief in its truth. Remedy: rescission only (court may award damages in lieu under s.2(2)).
❓ Mistake — Three Types
Common MistakeBoth parties share the same mistaken assumption about a fundamental fact — e.g., both believe the subject matter exists, but it does not (Couturier v Hastie [1856]).
Mutual MistakeParties are at cross purposes — each has a different understanding of the contract's subject matter. No binding agreement may exist (Raffles v Wichelhaus [1864]).
Unilateral MistakeOnly one party is mistaken. Generally does not void the contract unless the other party knew of and exploited the mistake (Smith v Hughes [1871]).
Remedies for Flawed Consent: Rescission (unwinding) · Damages (monetary compensation) · Rectification (court corrects document to reflect true agreement)
👶 Minors & Capacity to Consent
AgeLegal Position
Under 13Parental consent required for processing personal data (GDPR/UK GDPR Art. 8)

Under 18Minors lack contractual capacity. Contracts generally voidable at minor's option. Exceptions: necessaries (food, clothing, lodging).

💡 Gillick Competence (UK): A child under 16 may consent to medical treatment if they have "sufficient maturity and intelligence to understand the nature and implications" of the proposed treatment (Gillick v West Norfolk AHA [1986]).
🧠 Mental Capacity & the Four-Part Test
The Mental Capacity Act 2005 (UK) presumes capacity. A person lacks capacity only if they cannot make a decision for themselves due to an impairment of mind or brain.
1
Understand

Comprehend the information relevant to the decision, including consequences.

2
Retain

Hold the information long enough to make a decision.

3
Weigh Up

Use and evaluate the information to arrive at a choice.

4
Communicate

Communicate the decision by any means — speech, sign, or gesture.

⚠️ Temporary incapacity due to alcohol or drugs can vitiate consent. Contracts made while severely intoxicated may be voidable if the other party was aware of the incapacity.
↩️ Withdrawal of Consent — Key Rule & Contexts
Core rule: A person who has given consent retains the right to withdraw it at any time. Withdrawal does NOT negate the lawfulness of anything done before the withdrawal — but it ends authority for future action.
ContextWithdrawal RuleKey Case/Law
Medical TreatmentPatient can withdraw at any time, even mid-procedure (with capacity). Continuing without consent = battery.Re MB [1997]
GDPR Data ProcessingWithdrawal must be as easy as giving consent. Processing after withdrawal is unlawful. Prior processing remains lawful.GDPR Article 13
Sexual ConsentConsent must be present throughout and can be withdrawn at any moment. Continuing after withdrawal = assault.R v Bree [2007]
ContractsWithdrawal may amount to breach. Withdrawing party may face liability for damages unless a lawful termination right exists.General contract law
🔐 GDPR — Six Lawful Bases for Processing Data (Art. 6)
1. ConsentFreely given, specific, informed, unambiguous. Must be as easy to withdraw as to give.
2. ContractNecessary for performance of or steps prior to entering a contract with the data subject.
3. Legal ObligationRequired to comply with a legal obligation the controller is subject to.
4. Vital InterestsNecessary to protect someone's life — an emergency basis.
5. Public TaskNecessary for the exercise of official authority or public interest task.
6. Legitimate InterestsNecessary for the legitimate interests of the controller or a third party, not overridden by data subject rights.
📋 GDPR Consent Requirements (Article 7): Freely given (no detriment for refusal) · Specific & informed (clear purpose) · Unambiguous (positive opt-in — pre-ticked boxes are NOT valid) · Easy withdrawal (as easy as giving consent, takes effect going forward only).
Part 03 — When Consent Is Not Essential: Hume, Rawls & Sandel
🏠 Hume's House — Benefit-Based Obligation
Key tension: Can an obligation arise from benefit alone — even without any act of consent?
The FactsHume rented his Edinburgh house to Boswell, who sublet to a tenant. The tenant hired a contractor to make repairs — without consulting Hume. The contractor sent the bill to Hume. Hume refused: he had not consented.
The RulingThe court ordered Hume to pay. The benefit had been conferred; consent was not essential to create the obligation.
Hume's Objection"By the same rule, he may go and do what he thinks proper to be done at every house in Edinburgh, without the landlord's consent." — The losing argument.
🚗 Sam's Mobile Repair & Squeegee Men — The Limits
Benefit-based obligation has limits. Imposing unsolicited "services" in public spaces blurs the line with coercion.
Sam's ErrorSam began fixing a car without agreement then demanded $50/hour "whether I fix it or not." He confused consent with obligation. Benefit → obligation; not obligation → agreement.
Squeegee Men (NYC 1980s–90s)Men would wash windshields uninvited then demand payment — invoking benefit-based obligation. Giuliani ordered police to arrest them: benefit without consent is not sufficient basis for legal obligation in public spaces.
📜 Two Sources of Moral Obligation
Pure Consent-BasedReciprocity-Based
Obligation arises only from voluntary agreement. No consent = no duty. Libertarian view: uphold whatever consenting adults agree to. Obligation can arise from benefit alone — from the moral weight of fairness and fair return. No explicit agreement is required; reciprocity has independent moral force.
Sam's van · Squeegee men Hume's House · Contractor's bill
🎭 Rawls — The Veil of Ignorance
Actual contracts always risk unfairness: unequal bargaining power, superior knowledge, coercion, or deception. A signed agreement is not enough to guarantee a just one.
The VeilImagine parties equal in power and knowledge, choosing principles from behind a veil — not knowing their place in society, their talents, or their values.
The IronyA hypothetical agreement behind the veil is NOT morally weaker than an actual contract — it is morally stronger, because it is purified of every arbitrary advantage.
💡 Key insight: Contracts draw force from both autonomy (consent) AND reciprocity (fairness). Real moral life cannot be reduced to either alone. Even if you sign, we can still ask: is it fair?
📌 Key Principles of Consent — Summary
  • Consent must be voluntary, informed, specific, and given by a person with capacity — any element missing makes it flawed.
  • Duress, undue influence, misrepresentation, and mistake all vitiate consent and may void or render contracts voidable.
  • Consent without obligation means no penalty for refusal — bundled or coerced consent is not valid under law or GDPR.
  • Minors generally lack contractual capacity. Under-16s may give medical consent only if Gillick-competent.
  • Mental capacity is presumed. A person lacking capacity cannot give legally valid consent — others must act in their best interests.
  • Consent can be withdrawn at any time. Withdrawal does not make prior lawful processing unlawful but ends future authority.
  • GDPR sets a high bar: positive opt-in, specific purpose, easy withdrawal, no conditioning of services on unnecessary consent.
Part 04 — Confidentiality & IT Ethics
🔒 What is Confidentiality?
Psychologists/IT professionals have a primary obligation and take reasonable precautions to protect confidential information obtained in the course of their work.
TrustClients have the right to control what information is shared — it forms the foundation of effective therapy and professional assessment.
AutonomyClients determine what they share and to whom. Confidentiality respects the individual's right to information self-determination.
Legal DutyConfidentiality is encoded in law (HIPAA, GDPR) and enforceable professional codes — not just an ethical aspiration.
⚠️ Mandatory Exceptions — When Confidentiality Can Be Broken
Child AbuseAll states/jurisdictions mandate reporting of known or reasonably suspected child abuse or neglect — no professional discretion is permitted.
Duty to WarnTarasoff-derived duty: take reasonable steps to protect identifiable third parties from credible, serious threats (Tarasoff v. Regents, California [1976]).
Court OrdersA subpoena alone may not require disclosure; a court order does. Seek to limit scope before complying.
Danger to SelfImminent suicidal risk may require breaking confidentiality to hospitalise, contact next of kin, or alert emergency services.
💡 Key principle: Disclose only the minimum information necessary to fulfil the legal or safety obligation — never the full dataset.

📖 Key Definitions
BriberyAn inducement for an action which is illegal, unethical, or a breach of trust. Includes: cash, gifts, hospitality, payments to intermediaries, and favourable decisions. ACA 2023: "gratification" defined broadly to include non-monetary benefits, loans, favours, and promises.

❌ Common Misconceptions

  • ❌ "Only public officials can commit bribery" — FCPA and ACA cover private sector too.
  • ❌ "Using cryptocurrency makes payments untraceable and legal" — crypto exchanges are under FATF-compliant AML rules; increasingly traceable.
  • ❌ "Online gifts and digital vouchers are not bribes" — digital gifts, vouchers, data access, and software licences all constitute "gratification" under ACA 2023.

🇱🇰 Sri Lanka Anti-Corruption Act No.9 of 2023 — Key Provisions

Bribery Offences

Active bribery (giving), passive bribery (receiving), and attempt to bribe — all criminalised. Covers cash, gifts, services, favourable decisions, and digital currency.

Abuse of Function

Public officials who use their position to confer improper advantages are guilty. Includes manipulation of digital government systems. Aligned with UNCAC Article 19.

Illicit Enrichment

Public officials whose assets cannot be explained by lawful income are presumed guilty unless they prove otherwise. Burden of proof shifts to the accused.

Trading in Influence

Selling influence over decision-makers now explicitly criminalised. Closes a gap that existed in the 1954 Act. Applies to both the influencer and the buyer.

WB Protection

Informers, witnesses, and whistleblowers assisting CIABOC are protected from retaliation. Reports can be made orally, in writing, or electronically via the CIABOC online portal.

Assets Declaration

All public officials must periodically declare assets and liabilities on the Centralised Electronic Declaration System (launched March 2024). Non-compliance is a criminal offence.

Accept but keep it low-key under another department's budget WRONG — Concealment compounds the offence. Falsifying records adds a record-keeping violation. ACA 2023 criminalises exactly this conduct.

Accept but stay within the ministry's hospitality spending limit WRONG — The issue is not value but the relationship between the benefit and the pending decision. No internal spending limit creates a defence where corrupt intent is present.
😶 Unwitting Participant

Hoodwinked or negligent — does not realise they are facilitating corruption.

Example: Approving an agent invoice coded as "marketing expenses" without questioning the lack of service evidence.

Liability: May still apply — "should have known" is a recognised standard under ACA 2023.
😑 Witting Participant

Wilful blindness or connivance — knows something is wrong but turns a blind eye.

Example: Being told "don't make this a compliance issue" about suspicious digital payments; ignoring procurement approvals made via WhatsApp only.

Criminal risk: Wilful blindness can be treated as knowledge under ACA 2023.
😈 Willing Participant

Active collaboration or leadership — deliberately participates in or drives corrupt behaviour.

Example: Structuring crypto payments across multiple e-wallets to avoid CIABOC detection; concealing a bribe via complex ERP structures.

Full personal criminal prosecution — up to 7 years imprisonment and asset forfeiture under ACA 2023.

Part 01 — Deviance, Stigma & Crime: Core Definitions
📖 Three Core Definitions
DEVIANCEThe violation of social norms. We are all deviant at some time or another and in some places — deviance is not absolute but contextual.
STIGMAThe disapproval attached to disobeying the expected norms — a mark of social disgrace that sets the deviant apart from the rest of society. (Goffman)
CRIMEThe forms of deviance in which formal penalties are imposed by society. Any act prohibited by law and punishable by the government. "Crime is any act labelled as such by those in authority."
⚙️ Four Key Properties of Deviance
Ever-ChangingDeviance is not fixed — it shifts with time and culture. What was deviant in 1950 may be accepted today, and vice versa.
Socially ConstructedEven behaviours characterised as immoral absolutes are ultimately socially constructed — society determines what is deviant AND the consequences.
Culturally LocatedDeviance is culturally dependent and always exists in juxtaposition with some concept of "normality." What is deviant varies from society to society.
Context DependentDeviance is overlooked in some situations — context and social status greatly influence whether a behaviour is labelled deviant.
🏷️ Status & Deviance
Status powerfully shapes who gets labelled deviant — even for identical behaviours.
  • Low-status persons (ethnic minorities, poor) are MORE likely to be perceived as deviant
  • Higher-status persons (priests, doctors, executives) are LESS likely to be defined as deviant
  • Both acts AND individuals — even entire groups — can be defined as deviant
  • Deviance is not just about what you do — it's about who you are and who has the power to label you
🔄 Becoming a Deviant
Three key pathways to being labelled deviant:
1
Repeat behaviour

Not a one-off act but a recognisable pattern of norm violation over time.

2
Serious negative consequences

Committing an act with severe social impact — rape, murder, robbery, fraud.

3
Being detected and stigmatised

Discovery + social labelling = deviant identity. The label matters as much as the act.

⚙️ Social Functions of Deviance — Émile Durkheim
Social CohesionDraws the line between members and "outsiders" — bonds people through shared rejection of deviance. Creates in-group solidarity.
Norm ClarificationDefines the boundaries of acceptable behaviour — sets examples of unacceptable conduct, making norms visible and explicit.
Tension ReleaseActs that allow individuals to relieve tension without disrupting the basic fabric of society (e.g., acceptable protest, satire).
Social ChangeCan signal problems in a society that need to be addressed — stimulates positive change. Many social movements began as "deviant."
EmploymentCreates legitimate employment: judges, lawyers, police, prison personnel, criminologists, and crime reporters.
InnovationOpens societies to new and creative paths of thinking — innovation can emerge from challenging established norms.

Part 02 — Theories of Deviance: Six Major Frameworks
📐 Structural-Strain Theory — Robert Merton
American society places high value on certain goals (success, materialism), but not everyone has access to the legitimate means to achieve them. Under the strain of incompatible goals and means, individuals may fall into ANOMIE — a state of normlessness and confusion when norms are unclear or no longer applicable.

Ritualism❌ Reject✅ AcceptDisengaged teacher follows rules — gave up on goals but keeps doing the job

🔗 Social Control Theory — Hirschi
Our bonds with society encourage us to conform. With fewer bonds, we are more likely to be deviant.
AttachmentStronger attachment to groups/individuals → more likely to conform
CommitmentGreater commitment to social goals → more likely to conform
InvolvementParticipation in approved social activities increases conformity
BeliefBelief in norms and values of society promotes conformity
📚 Differential Association — Sutherland
Conformity or deviance is LEARNED from those we spend time with.
  • Ratio: Number of deviant vs. non-deviant influences in one's social network
  • Significance: How important/respected the person modelling the behaviour is
  • Age of exposure: Earlier exposure to deviant models has stronger effect
  • Deviance is not chosen — it is socialised
⚖️ Rational Choice Theory
The decision to be deviant depends on a cost/benefit analysis of sanctions.
  • People weigh potential gains against potential punishment
  • When expected benefits exceed expected costs → deviance occurs
  • Deterrence works by raising the cost side of the equation
  • Assumes individuals are rational, calculating actors
🏷️ Labelling Theory — Howard Becker
"Behaviour is not intrinsically deviant but becomes deviant because it is labelled as such." — Howard Becker, Outsiders (1963)
Primary DevianceInitial norm violation — not yet labelled. The person does not yet see themselves as deviant.
Secondary DevianceLife becomes organised around breaking the norm and managing the label — the deviant identity is adopted.
Master StatusThe deviant label overrides all other identities — "once a criminal, always a criminal." The label becomes the person.
Increased AlienationLabel pushes the individual further from mainstream society and support systems.
Deviant Peer ExposureForces increased interaction with other deviant peers, reinforcing deviant identity.
Self-Fulfilling ProphecySociety's expectations of the labelled individual shape their future behaviour and life chances. The label becomes reality.
💰 Conflict Theory
  • Deviance is a result of social inequality — elites define what is deviant to benefit themselves
  • Ruling class labels threatening behaviour as deviant to protect their interests
  • Law enforcement directed toward crimes of lower classes → higher arrest rates for the poor
  • Greater power differentials = greater conflict in society
  • Conflict is inevitable under capitalist power arrangements
♀️ Feminist Theory of Deviance & Crime
  • Abuses suffered by women are rooted in the patriarchal, capitalist system
  • Causes include: gendered division of labour, separate spheres, socialisation of children
  • Cultural attitudes toward crime against women differ by women's status in that society
  • Women are less often in a position to commit crimes
  • Women's crimes differ in nature from men's — shaped by gendered social context
⚡ Shaming Theory — Braithwaite
Disintegrative ShamingReintegrative Shaming
Wrongdoer is stigmatised, rejected, banished from conventional society. Tends to LEAD to MORE deviance. Wrongdoer feels guilt, yet is shown forgiveness and understanding. Maintains relationship while condemning the act. DISCOURAGES further deviance.
Example: Public shaming, prison, lifelong criminal record Example: Family intervention, restorative conference, conditional discharge

Part 03 — Types of Crime & The Criminal Justice System
⚖️ Six Types of Crime

Violent / Street Crime

Murder, robbery, assault. Most visible but smallest percentage of all crimes. Most victims in the US are African Americans. Includes robbery (larceny by violence/threat).

Property Crime

Stealing or damaging others' property — burglary, larceny, vehicle theft, arson. More common — occurs once every 3 seconds in the US.

White-Collar Crime

Committed by individuals of high status. Fraud, embezzlement, tax evasion, wage theft. LESS publicised but MORE costly — estimated $300 billion annually in the US. Less deadly in appearance, more systemic in impact.

Organised Crime

Pursuit of crime as a big business — crime syndicates use legitimate businesses as fronts, operating through violence or its threat. Complex hierarchical organisations.

Hate Crimes

Crimes motivated by bias against race, religion, gender, sexuality or other characteristics of the victim. Target an identity, not just an individual.

State-Organised Crime

Crimes committed by or with the complicity of government agencies — war crimes, corruption, state surveillance abuses. Often the hardest to prosecute.

📏 Three Ways of Measuring Crime
UCRUniform Crime Reports — Published annually by the FBI. Charts crimes reported to police. Limits: not all crime is reported (especially family/friend crimes), officers may be influenced by the attitude of the complainant.
Self-Report SurveysIndividuals report their own deviant or criminal behaviour anonymously. Captures crimes that never reach police — useful for victimless crimes and underreported offences.
Victimisation SurveysAsk members of the public whether they have been victims of crime (whether or not they reported it). Provides a fuller picture than official stats.
🔺 Triangulation is best — using all three methods together provides the most accurate picture of crime.
🏛️ The Criminal Justice System — Four Components
POLICEHave most immediate control over who is arrested. Police discretion and racial profiling are key issues. Factors: seriousness of offence, wishes of victim, attitude of suspect, race of suspect.
COURTSDetermine guilt or innocence by trial. 90% of cases settled through plea bargaining — negotiating a guilty plea for a lesser charge to reduce caseloads.
CORRECTIONSSanctions (prison, parole, probation) serving four functions: Retribution · Deterrence · Rehabilitation · Social Protection. Recidivism rate: 62% charged with new crimes within 3 years.
JUVENILE JUSTICEFor offenders under 18. Guarantees same legal rights as adults. Often provides more rehabilitative services. Focused on reform rather than punishment.

Part 04 — Restorative Justice
🤝 What is Restorative Justice?
"Restorative Justice is a process whereby the parties with a stake in a particular offense come together to collectively resolve how to deal with the aftermath of the offense, repair the harm and its implications for the future."
— Howard Zehr
Traditional Justice Asks…Restorative Justice Asks (Howard Zehr)…
Who committed the crime?What is the harm?
What laws were broken?What needs to be done to repair the harm?
How will we punish the offender?Who is responsible for this repair?
💡 Restorative Justice is NOT a programme — it is a philosophical framework and a different way of responding to crime. It asks us to rethink the roles of government and community in achieving justice.
💊 Principle 1 — Healing

Justice requires that we work to heal victims, communities, and offenders who have been injured by crime. Crime is a wound — justice should be healing.

🤝 Principle 2 — Involvement

Victims, communities, and offenders should have opportunities for active involvement in the justice process as early and as fully as possible.

🏛️ Principle 3 — Roles

We must rethink the roles of government and community. Government preserves a just order; community establishes a just peace.

⚖️ Retributive vs. Restorative Justice — Comparison
Retributive JusticeRestorative Justice
Crime is a violation of the state and its lawsCrime is a violation against a person and the community
Focuses on establishing guilt and delivering punishmentFocuses on repairing harm and shared interests of victim, offender, and community
Victim and offender play passive roles in an adversarial processAll parties are active participants — dialogue replaces adversarial process
Both parties lose — victim is sidelined; offender is stigmatisedBoth parties can win — victims receive acknowledgement; offenders accept responsibility
High recidivism (62%) — punishment alone rarely repairs harmSuccess measured by repair done and restored relationships — not severity of punishment
🔗 Links to Braithwaite's Reintegrative Shaming Theory: Restorative Justice uses reintegrative shaming — discouraging deviant behaviour without stigmatising the person. It condemns the act, not the actor.
